Zagrajmy · branding 01

A logo worth
playing for._

Research-driven logo concepts for Zagrajmy — the Polish tabletop & convention platform. Not a rebrand: a mark that finally matches the product's warm, confident design language.

Text-first pitch → pick a direction → generate with image models → vectorize → rebuild in real type.

Where we are 02

We currently have two logos, and neither is doing the job.

The stacked LUDAMUS mark

Three clashing hues (purple, yellow, teal) that exist nowhere in the product palette, drop shadows that die at small sizes, raster-only, and it spells Ludamus while the brand users see is Zagrajmy.

The navbar cube

Closer — flat, geometric, coral. But its coral (#F05537) doesn't match the token (#f85a3c), the isometric cube reads as "generic tech/crypto box", and it was never designed as a system with the wordmark, favicon, or og-image.

Diagnosis: no single mark passes the basic professional tests — one-color survival, 16 px legibility, palette fit, name match.

Research · principles 03

What the best logo designers actually teach

"A logo derives meaning from the quality of the thing it symbolizes, not the other way around."

— Paul Rand (IBM, UPS, ABC)

Sagi Haviv · CG&H

Three criteria, nothing else: appropriate to the brand, distinctive enough to be remembered, simple enough to survive every size and medium. A logo shouldn't carry the whole brand story.

Michael Bierut · Pentagram

A new logo is "an empty vessel awaiting the meaning that will be poured into it by history and experience." Preloaded symbolism (eagles, globes… meeples) only limits what the mark can become.

Lindon Leader · FedEx

"Simplicity and clarity. Great design is born of those two things." The famous arrow wasn't briefed — it was discovered in iteration 200+. Rigorous typographic iteration beats symbol-first illustration.

Saul Bass · AT&T

"Pushed to its utmost limits in terms of abstraction and ambiguity, yet still readable." If it's simple-simple, it's boring — aim for the idea that makes you think and rethink.

Paul Rand · the 7-step test

Score distinctiveness, visibility, adaptability, memorability, universality, timelessness 1–10 each — and simplicity 1–15. Reduction is the heaviest-weighted discipline, not a nice-to-have.

Aaron Draplin · DDC

Anti-precious pragmatism: sketch exhaustively before touching vectors, show thick and thin, and "let me make you something that is yours" — not a designer's signature style.

Research · landscape 04

The space splits into two poles — and the middle is empty.

Gaming platforms are either corporate-serious (Roll20's black wordmark, Gen Con's no-effects rules, PAX minimalism) or chaotic-fun (Partiful's sticker maximalism, clip-art dice clusters). Event platforms own orange (Meetup, Eventbrite) or neon purple (Luma). Nobody owns warm, confident, communal — which is exactly where our design system already lives.

Brand foundations 05

The name is already an invitation. The mark must extend it.

Zagrajmy = "let's play" — first-person plural, a hand extended. The engine is even named the same thing in Latin: ludamus, "let us play". The brand's one idea is communal invitation, and the product's mechanics are tables, seats, and schedules.

Typeface

Outfit (400–800) is the product's only face. The wordmark should be built from it — customized, not replaced.

Color

Coral #f85a3c as the single accent on warm paper (#fdfcfb/#f7f5f3), ink #252220, teal #14b89b reserved for secondary UI — the mark itself stays one-accent.

Shape language

The whole UI renders with corner-shape: superellipse(1.8). The squircle is our native geometry — the logo should be built from it.

Constraint

Not a rebrand. The mark must drop into today's navbar, favicon and og:image without touching the rest of the system.

The brief 06

Every concept must pass six gates before we generate a pixel.

  1. 16 px test — reads as one clear element at favicon size and on a conference badge from across the room.
  2. One-color test — works as solid ink and reversed white before color is even considered. No gradients, ever.
  3. Combination mark — new brands need the name legible: symbol + "zagrajmy" wordmark, with the symbol able to stand alone later once equity accrues.
  4. Cliché gate — nothing from the blacklist. Abstraction over literal game objects.
  5. System fit — token coral, Outfit, superellipse geometry; drops into the current UI unchanged.
  6. Vessel test — per Bierut: simple enough to acquire meaning, not so literal it explains itself to death.
Concept A 07

Stół — the table, seen from above.

A coral superellipse tabletop with four seats — the one image every tabletop genre shares (board games, RPG, cards) without depicting any single one of them. It's literally what the product does: puts people at tables.

Why it wins: gathering without meeples; reads at 16 px (favicon = table+seats blob resolves cleanly); squircle geometry is straight from the UI. Bass-style productive ambiguity: table, plus-sign, compass rose, four players joining.
Risk: four-seat symmetry can skew "hospital plus" in one color — seat length/radius needs iteration.

flat vector logo, abstract top-down table with four rounded seats, rounded superellipse square, minimalist, simple geometric shapes, solid colors, icon only, centered composition, white background, using only #f85a3c and #252220, no gradients, no 3D, no drop shadows, no text, clean edges
Concept B 08

W grze — the grid, with one tile in play.

Four squircle tiles — a timetable grid, a game board, a hall of tables — with one tile tilted and coral: the session that's live, the piece being played, the person who just joined. The mark is derived from the product's actual mechanic (schedule grids), which the landscape research flagged as unclaimed territory.

Why it wins: order + one act of play = the whole brand in one gesture; genuinely ownable (no one in the niche has it); the tilted tile animates beautifully (hover: tile snaps into play).
Risk: reads "app grid / dashboard" if the tilt is too timid — the rotation angle is the design.

flat vector logo, four rounded squares in a 2x2 grid, one square tilted 15 degrees breaking the grid, minimalist, geometric, solid colors, icon only, centered composition, white background, using only #f85a3c and #252220, no gradients, no 3D, no shadows, no text, clean edges
Concept C 09

Zetka — a Z that moves forward.

A geometric Z monogram in a coral superellipse tile. The diagonal is drawn as a path — the zigzag route through a convention hall, a turn passing from player to player. Very few brands own a Z; in Poland the letter instantly keys to the name.

Why it wins: strongest 16 px performance of all concepts (app-icon-native); Leader-style potential for a discovered negative-space arrow in the diagonal; trivially drops into the navbar today.
Risk: monograms are the most "empty vessel" of vessels — it says nothing about play until the brand pours meaning in (Bierut would call that a feature).

flat vector logo, bold geometric letter Z monogram inside rounded superellipse app icon tile, angular zigzag path, minimalist, solid colors, icon only, centered composition, white background, using only #f85a3c and #fdfcfb, no gradients, no 3D, no shadows, clean edges, modern geometric sans-serif style
Concept D 10

Wykrzyknik — the wordmark that shouts politely.

Wordmark-first: lowercase zagrajmy set in customized Outfit, closed with a coral exclamation mark that leans forward — the invitation made typographic. The grammar does the work: the name is the call to action.

Why it wins: maximum name legibility (the research consensus for young brands); Leader-style iteration surface — letterspacing, the j–y descender rhythm, a discovered ligature could become the real mark; the "!" tile alone becomes the favicon.
Risk: "!" can feel pushy if overweighted, and a wordmark alone leaves no strong square symbol until the "!" earns recognition.

flat vector wordmark logo, lowercase word "zagrajmy" in modern geometric sans-serif, tilted rounded exclamation mark accent after the word, minimalist typography logo, solid colors, white background, using only #252220 and #f85a3c, no gradients, no 3D, no shadows, clean edges, perfect letter spacing
Concept E 11

Kostka 2.0 — evolve the cube we already ship.

The conservative option: keep the navbar's isometric cube but design it properly — token coral, warm ink instead of pure black, softened corners to match the superellipse language, and a real lockup with the wordmark. A cube is a die with the pips removed: the one game object abstract enough to survive the cliché gate.

Why it wins: zero migration cost, continuity for existing users, honest "evolution not revolution".
Risk: isometric cubes are the tech-startup cliché outside gaming; opacity-tint one-color fallback is a structural weakness the other concepts don't have. Weakest differentiation of the five.

flat vector logo, minimal isometric cube with three faces, rounded corners, one face coral, minimalist, geometric, solid colors, icon only, centered composition, white background, using only #f85a3c, #252220 and #3d3732, no gradients, no pips, no 3D rendering, no shadows, clean edges
Concept F · wordmark 12

Graj — the imperative hiding inside the name.

The name decomposes into real Polish words: za + graj + my — it contains gra (game), graj (play!) and my (we). Set the whole word in one confident weight and let color alone pick out graj — the wordmark carries its own call to action. The FedEx lesson: the strongest idea was already inside the letterforms.

Why it wins: instantly legible to every Polish speaker, pure typography to everyone else; no symbol needed to explain the brand; a single weight keeps the word calm and even-textured. In one color the emphasis survives as a rounded rule under graj — which doubles as the table the UI can "sit" on. Variant worth testing: emphasize my ("we") instead. Refinement layer: cut the counters of a/g/a as superellipses to match the UI's corner geometry.
Risk: color-only emphasis needs strong contrast management on dark grounds; needs concept G's tiers to work at 16 px.

flat vector wordmark logo, lowercase word "zagrajmy" in modern geometric sans-serif, single bold weight, letters "graj" in coral and the rest in dark warm ink, minimalist typography logo, white background, using only #f85a3c and #252220, no gradients, no 3D, no shadows, clean edges, tight letter spacing
Concept G · wordmark system 13

Teleskop — a name that shortens through real words.

The responsive-logo consensus says ship 3–4 tiers, each a deliberate redesign. Almost every brand amputates letters to get there — we get to shorten through real words: zagrajmy (full lockup) → graj (badges, avatars, merch) → z tile (16 px favicon, the Zetka geometry). Every tier still says something true.

Why it wins: linguistic luck no competitor can copy; solves F's small-size weakness instead of fighting it; unifies concepts F and C into one system with a single geometry and color logic.
Risk: three tiers must be designed as one family (stroke weights, corner radii) or it reads as three logos; "graj" alone needs context before the brand is known.

brand identity sheet, three logo lockups of one brand stacked vertically: word "zagrajmy" in single-weight geometric sans-serif with letters "graj" in coral, word "graj" alone in coral, single letter "z" white on rounded coral app icon tile, flat vector, minimalist, white background, using only #f85a3c and #252220, no gradients, no 3D, no shadows, clean edges
Concept H · wordmark 14

Stos — the stack, redeemed.

ZA / GRAJ / MY stacked flush-left in a single weight of Outfit — Polish poster-school discipline applied to the same gesture the old LUDAMUS logo attempted: a stacked name. One ink, one coral line, a strict grid. The word-square format is a ready-made og:image, conference badge and t-shirt print.

Why it wins: keeps continuity with the project's history instead of erasing it — the stack was never the problem, the execution was; naturally splits the name into its meaningful parts; Polish poster geometry is whitespace nobody in the niche claims.
Risk: square format is a poor fit for the horizontal navbar (needs a one-line companion); stacked type always sacrifices some quick legibility.

flat vector logo, stacked wordmark three lines flush left "ZA" "GRAJ" "MY" in single bold weight geometric sans-serif, middle line coral, other lines dark warm ink, Polish poster style typography, minimalist, white background, using only #f85a3c and #252220, no gradients, no 3D, no shadows, clean edges
Concept I · wordmark 15

Przeplot — fancysnake's interlock, developed.

The original stacked mark (today's og:image) had the right instinct: syllables piled up and interlocking like pieces settling on a table. What failed was execution — a palette from outside the product, drop shadows, raster-only. Developed: za / graj / my in Outfit 700, rows genuinely overlapping, colors from the system (ink, coral, teal — the one concept where teal earns a seat), flat knockout gaps where the shadows used to be.

Why it wins: keeps continuity with the project's first identity instead of erasing it; brings the playful energy the disciplined concepts deliberately lack; the interlock literally depicts "things coming together" — the product's whole job. It's the strongest og:image / poster / shirt format on the board.
Risk: three colors break the one-accent discipline every other concept obeys; the overlap dies below ~48 px, so it can only ever be the display tier of a system (pair with G's small tiers), never the favicon.

flat vector wordmark logo, three stacked lowercase words "za" "graj" "my" in single bold weight geometric sans-serif, rows overlapping and interlocking with thin white knockout gaps between letters, top word dark warm ink, middle word coral, bottom word teal, flat solid colors, white background, using only #252220, #f85a3c and #14b89b, no gradients, no 3D, no drop shadows, clean edges
Concept J · symbol 16

Zapis — the sword that signs you up.

The pre-Ludamus Zagrajmy logo has the best concept of any mark this brand has owned: a calendar crossed by a sword whose tip is a pencil — schedule the adventure, sign up for the quest. That's a FedEx-arrow-grade double reading. What buries it: three objects where Rand's test demands one, a d6 straight off the cliché blacklist, and fine line-art plus a sticker outline that vanish below 48 px.

The development: fuse everything into a single glyph — a flat sword-pencil crossing a superellipse calendar tile (binding tabs on top), solid system colors, die deleted. One object, two readings, per Bass: pushed toward abstraction, still readable.


Why it wins: the only pictorial concept that says both halves of the product — events and games; honors the original identity's idea while fixing its execution.
Risk: sword skews RPG-ward — board-game-only spheres may not see themselves in it; the fused glyph needs careful weight balance to avoid reading as "dagger app". The j-swap softens both: in the wordmark the sword is a typographic wink, not a genre flag.

flat vector logo, minimalist sword fused with pencil, blade ending in a pencil tip, crossing diagonally over a rounded square calendar tile with two binding tabs on top, simple geometric shapes, solid colors, icon only, centered composition, white background, using only #f85a3c, #252220 and #fdfcfb, no gradients, no 3D, no shadows, no dice, no fine detail, clean edges

Next steps 18

From prompt to production vector

  1. Generate — run the concept prompts through GPT-image / Ideogram v3 (best text & flat shapes), Recraft V3 (native SVG output — fastest path), and Qwen-Image (strongest text rendering). 10–20 variants per concept; always pass exact hex codes.
  2. Cull hard — keep only candidates with crisp edges and consistent geometry. Expect and reject: mushy boundaries, sneaky gradients, warped letters, mismatched "twin" shapes.
  3. Vectorize — Recraft's built-in vectorize or Vectorizer.ai for clean few-node paths; Illustrator Image Trace / Inkscape+potrace when we want manual control.
  4. Rebuild, don't trace, the type — the wordmark is reset in real Outfit and kerned by hand. AI output is "an image of a logo", never the logo. For the wordmark concepts (F–H) treat generations as proportion/color studies only — diffusion models warp Polish letterforms, so Ideogram or Qwen-Image first, and expect to discard the type either way.
  5. Correct optically — snap to a simple grid, then cheat it where the eye demands (overshoots, junction thinning). Every great gridded logo cheats the grid.
  6. Ship the system — 3 tiers: full lockup → symbol → 16 px favicon; each a deliberate redesign. Replace logo.png, favicon.ico, navbar SVG and og:image in one PR.
